At some point I realized we kind of needed a proper curtain rod in the kitchen.

For a while we were just using a piece of cloth stuck to the window with paper tape. It did the job, but I kept telling myself I'll fix it properly. And since I've been trying to make small improvements around the house lately, this one kept coming back.

I already had a metal rod that my dad gave me. Measured it, around 6 mm diameter, one meter long. Good enough. So the plan was simple: make some supports and mount everything directly on the PVC window frame.

## Designing the holders

I opened Tinkercad and started working on the holders. Went through a few versions, printed a couple of prototypes just to get the fit right.

![Small preview of the curtain rod holder design in Tinkercad](tinkercad-holder-design.svg)
_Placeholder: small preview of the holder design in Tinkercad._

In the end I kept it simple. Two pieces.

Left side closed, acts like a stopper. Right side open, so I can slide the rod in and out without thinking too much about it.

## Printing and cleanup

Printed them in white PETG from Sunlu. After printing there was some stringing, so I just took a small blowtorch and passed over them quickly. Gone.

![Printed white PETG holders with slight stringing](printed-holders-stringing.svg)
_Placeholder: printed holders with slight stringing._

![Cleaning PETG stringing with a small blowtorch](blowtorch-cleanup.svg)

## Mounting without drilling

For mounting, I didn't want to drill anything. I usually try to avoid that if I can. So I went with a strong double-sided tape, rated somewhere around 12 kg.

It's probably overkill, but I'd rather have it solid. If someone pulls on the cloth at some point, I don't want surprises.

![Close-up of strong double-sided mounting tape](double-sided-tape.svg)
_Placeholder: close-up of the strong double-sided mounting tape._

Cleaned the surface, degreased it, aligned everything with a small level, marked the position lightly. All good.

## Cutting the rod

Then I went to cut the rod.

Marked the middle, took it outside, clamped it on the Parkside workbench and cut it with the angle grinder.

And yeah, classic problem. Measure twice, cut once. I did measure once, cut once, and that was enough to get two slightly different pieces.

Not a huge difference, but enough to bother me. So I just trimmed them again and made them equal. Good enough.

![Cutting the metal rod on the workbench](cutting-metal-rod.svg)
_Placeholder: cutting the metal rod on the workbench._

## Final fit

Back inside, stuck the holders in place, pressed them well and left them there for a bit.

![3D printed holders mounted on the PVC window frame](mounted-holders-pvc.svg)
_Placeholder: holders mounted on the PVC window frame._

The rod fits nicely, everything feels solid. I threw the cloth on it quickly just to see how it looks. I'll arrange it properly later, but at least now it's not taped directly to the glass anymore.

![Final setup with rod mounted and cloth hanging](final-curtain-rod-cloth.svg)
_Placeholder: final setup with rod mounted and cloth hanging._

I'll also put the model on MakerWorld, so if anyone needs something similar, it's there.

One thing though: I placed the supports a bit closer than ideal. It works, but the textile feels a bit cramped. Each rod piece probably should have been around 50 cm or even a bit more.

We'll probably get used to it like this, but if I find a longer rod at some point, I might redo it and space them out a bit better.
